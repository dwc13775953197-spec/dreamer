# Walk #248

## External Material: EFPSA Cognitive Overload and Burnout Research (2026/05)

### Core Findings

1. **Cognitive overload != task count**. Feeling cognitively overloaded predicts burnout more reliably than working long hours (Sonnentag et al., 2010). The real driver is not how many tasks you have, but how many "mental tabs" occupy your attention.

2. **Attentional Residue**: When switching tasks, cognitive resources get "stuck" on the interrupted task. Even when returning to the primary task, part of your resources remains at the interruption (Mark et al., 2008). This creates a constant "partial attention" state that blocks deep focus and flow (Leroy, 2009; Monsell, 2003).

3. **Working Memory is Limited**: 4-7 items (Cowan, 2001). Piling too many tabs -> mental lag -> multiplying errors -> tasks feeling insurmountable.

4. **Three Types of Cognitive Load**:
   - Intrinsic: complexity of the task itself
   - Extraneous: distractions, interruptions, unclear instructions
   - Germane: effort to process information effectively
   
   Modern work environments pile extraneous load, forcing workers to waste energy on constant interruptions.

5. **Existential Impoverishment**: Burnout is not just exhaustion but "disruption of presence and lived experience." The self needs moments of stillness to reflect and integrate experience; without it, we become "a collection of scattered responses" rather than a cohesive individual (Baumeister, 1998; Killingsworth & Gilbert, 2010).

6. **Physiological Cost**: Sustained cognitive load triggers stress hormones like cortisol, impairing prefrontal decision-making and attention control (Lorist et al., 2005; McEwen, 2007). Cognitive overload also damages sleep, emotion, and immune function (Brosschot et al., 2006).
