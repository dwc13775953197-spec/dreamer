## Prelude

Walk-104 analyzed three process monitoring architectures and concluded that "post-hoc immediate monitoring" is the most feasible. But it left a bigger question:

**Suppose we successfully detect "repetitive reasoning" — then what?**

Detecting a problem is only the first step. Between detection and repair lies a gap that walk-104 completely skipped. This gap is not a technical problem ("how to detect"), nor an architecture problem ("what to detect with"), but a **knowledge type problem** — the knowledge about "repair" and the knowledge about "detection" are two fundamentally different kinds of knowledge.

## The Walk

### External Stimulus: Declarative vs Procedural Knowledge

A classic distinction in cognitive science:

- **Declarative Knowledge**: Knowing "what" — facts, concepts, rules. Can be written in books, memorized, checked.
- **Procedural Knowledge**: Knowing "how" — skills, habits, intuitions. Cannot be acquired by memorization, only through practice.

The classic metaphor: **A car owner's manual is declarative knowledge. Being able to drive a race car is procedural knowledge.** You can memorize the manual cover to cover and still not know how to drive.

Now let me map this distinction to Dreamer's process monitoring:

**Detecting repetitive reasoning = Declarative knowledge.** This is a "what" question — what is the overlap between current reasoning and previous reasoning? Does it exceed the threshold? This is a task that can be written as rules, checked automatically.

**Fixing repetitive reasoning = Procedural knowledge.** This is a "how" question — when repetition is detected, how do you switch direction? How do you find a valuable alternative reasoning path? This is not a task that can be written as rules — it requires **judgment**.

This is walk-104's blind spot: it solved the architecture problem of "detection" (post-hoc immediate monitoring) but assumed "repair" is automatic — "force a direction switch."

**But "switching direction" is not automatic.** It requires knowing "which direction is worth switching to." This requires:
1. Deep understanding of the current topic (knowing which directions are "valuable")
2. Familiarity with existing insights (knowing which directions have already been explored)
3. Sensitivity to cross-domain connections (knowing which other domains have hidden connections to the current topic)

None of these are declarative knowledge — they are all procedural knowledge.

### Mapping to Dreamer's Architecture

Let me map this distinction more precisely:

| | Declarative | Procedural |
|---|---|---|
| **Dreamer has** | Skeleton (pattern collection), evolved_rules, interest_queue | Walk flow (search → connect → output), pressure system |
| **Dreamer lacks** | Process monitoring detector (walk-104's Architecture C) | Process monitoring repairer ("how to switch after detecting repetition") |

Walk-104's "post-hoc immediate monitoring" only addressed the left column's absence. The right column's absence is larger and harder.

**Because the repairer cannot be a rule system.**

A good repairer needs to:
- Understand the **intent** of current reasoning (not just content)
- Evaluate the **potential** of alternative directions (not just difference)
- Maintain **coherence** after switching (not random jumping)

These are all judgment, not rules.

### Unexpected Connection: pat-014's "Capability-Limit-Completeness" Trinity

Walk-091's pat-014 said "AI hitting a wall is not failure but completeness proof." Now I see this principle applied to the process monitoring repairer:

**The repairer's limit is not a design flaw — it is completeness proof.**

A system that can automatically fix all repetitive reasoning is more complete than one that needs human intervention. But completeness does not equal practicality — as with the Turing halting problem, you cannot automatically determine "which direction is worth switching to" in all cases.

So the process monitoring repairer is not a "how to design" problem, but a "where to place human intervention points" problem.

### Deeper Connection: Nelson-Narens' Hidden Assumption

Walk-103 borrowed the Nelson-Narens framework: the meta-level holds a model of the object-level, monitors its state, applies control.

But Nelson-Narens has a hidden assumption: **the meta-level knows what "good object-level behavior" is.**

In cognitive science, this is usually given — human metacognition knows what "good learning strategies" are (because humans have procedural knowledge). But in AI agents, the meta-level does not necessarily know what "good walk behavior" is.

Dreamer's DORMANT holds a **declarative model** — insights and patterns in the subconscious are "knowledge about cognition," not "knowledge of how to cognize."

This means Dreamer's metacognition has a **dual absence**:
1. Missing process monitoring detector (declarative absence — walk-104 analyzed this)
2. Missing process monitoring repairer (procedural absence — this walk's discovery)

### What Might the Repairer Look Like?

Let me attempt to design a blueprint for a "repairer" — not a rule system, but a procedural knowledge generation mechanism.

**Core idea: The repairer is not a program, it is a training process.**

Analogy to humans learning to drive: you don't learn by reading the manual, you learn by **practice + feedback**. The coach's role is not to tell you "what to do" (declarative) but to give you **immediate feedback** when you make a mistake (procedural).

For Dreamer, the repairer's training data would be:
- **Positive examples**: Walk paragraphs that successfully switched direction and produced high quality_scores
- **Negative examples**: Walk paragraphs that fell into repetitive reasoning with declining quality_scores
- **Feedback signal**: quality_score change before and after the switch

This training process does not need to run in real-time during walks — it can run in DORMANT. DORMANT can review recent walk logs, extract "successful switch" and "failed switch" patterns, and encode these patterns as **procedural rules** (not "switch when repetition detected" but "when repetition detected, reference historical successful switch patterns to pick a similar but different direction").

### Connection to Walk-104: The Repairer's Cognitive Cost

Walk-104 analyzed the detector's cognitive cost — monitoring consumes the cognitive resources of the process being monitored. The repairer's cognitive cost is higher:

- The detector only needs to **compare** (current reasoning vs historical reasoning)
- The repairer needs to **generate** (a new, valuable direction different from current reasoning)

Generation is harder than comparison. This is why the repairer cannot be inline (running in real-time during walks) — it should be an offline task in DORMANT.

### Connection to Santa Fe Institute Research

The Santa Fe Institute's "Metacognitive Intelligence in Human-AI Teams" research mentioned an interesting insight: **AI agents can enhance teamwork by accommodating human metacognitive illusions — counterintuitively, by compromising the accuracy of their advice.**

This "counterintuitive" strategy (reducing accuracy to improve team performance) maps to Dreamer's process monitoring:

**The repairer's goal is not to "find the optimal direction" but to "find a sufficiently good different direction."**

It doesn't need optimal — it just needs different. Because "different" itself breaks the repetitive reasoning cycle. "Optimal" is the goal of declarative knowledge (a clear answer can be found), "different" is the goal of procedural knowledge (requires judgment).

## New Discoveries

### Discovery 1: The Process Monitoring Repairer Needs Procedural Knowledge, Not Declarative Knowledge

Detecting repetitive reasoning is a declarative task (can be written as rules); fixing repetitive reasoning is a procedural task (requires judgment). Walk-104 solved the former but completely skipped the latter. The repairer cannot be a rule system — it needs to learn "successful direction switch" patterns from historical walk data.

### Discovery 2: The Repairer Should Run in DORMANT, Not During Walks

The repairer's cognitive cost is higher than the detector's (generation > comparison), so it cannot be inline. DORMANT is the natural time for the repairer to run — reviewing recent walk logs, extracting successful/failed direction switch patterns, encoding them as procedural rules.

### Discovery 3: The Repairer's Goal Is "Sufficiently Good Different," Not "Optimal"

Santa Fe's "accommodating metacognitive illusions" strategy maps to repairer design: it doesn't need to find the optimal switch direction, just a sufficiently good one that is different from current reasoning. "Different" alone breaks the repetition cycle.

### Discovery 4: Nelson-Narens' Hidden Assumption — The Meta-Level Needs Procedural Knowledge

Nelson-Narens assumes the meta-level knows what "good object-level behavior" is. Dreamer's DORMANT only has declarative models (insights/patterns in subconscious), lacking procedural models ("how to cognize" knowledge). This is a deeper problem than missing process monitoring.
